## What Is a Cold Air Intake and Why Does It Matter?

A cold air intake relocates your engine's air filter away from the hot engine bay and replaces the restrictive factory airbox with a smoother, larger-diameter tube. The result: **denser, cooler air** reaches the combustion chamber, producing more power with every stroke.

Stock intake systems are designed around noise, cost, and emissions — not performance. Swapping to a cold air intake is one of the fastest ways to free up hidden horsepower your engine already has.

### How Much Power Can You Expect?

Gains vary by platform, but real-world dyno pulls typically show:

- **5–15 whp** on naturally aspirated engines
- **10–25 whp** on forced-induction builds (where airflow is even more critical)
- Noticeable improvement in throttle response and induction sound

## Installation Tips

Installing a cold air intake is typically a **1–2 hour DIY job** requiring only basic hand tools. Follow these steps for a clean install:

1. **Disconnect the battery** before touching any electrical connections near the intake
2. Remove the factory airbox and all attached ducting
3. Unplug the **MAF sensor** carefully — this is your most expensive component
4. Route the new intake tube following the included template
5. Secure all clamps to **4–5 Nm** — over-tightening cracks silicone couplers
6. Clear any fault codes and let the ECU relearn with a 20-minute drive cycle

> **Pro tip:** After installation, always check for vacuum leaks by listening for a whistling sound at idle. A cheap smoke tester removes all doubt.

## Oiled vs. Dry Filter: Which Should You Choose?

| Feature | Oiled Filter | Dry Filter |
|---|---|---|
| Filtration | Excellent | Very good |
| Airflow | Slightly higher | Slightly lower |
| Maintenance | Re-oil every 30k mi | Wash only |
| MAF safety | Risk if over-oiled | No risk |

For most street builds, **dry filters win on convenience**. For maximum filtration on dusty roads or track environments, oiled filters remain the gold standard.
